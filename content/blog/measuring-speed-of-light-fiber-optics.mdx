# Measuring the Speed of Light with a 20-Meter Fiber

Most people know the speed of light in a vacuum is a staggering $299,792,458$ m/s. But what happens when you force that light through a tiny strand of glass? 

In our latest **Modern Physics Laboratory** session at Marmara University we set out to measure this speed not across the vastness of space but across a 18.9-meter optical fiber sitting right on our workbench.

## The Challenge: Catching the Fastest Thing in the Universe

Measuring something that travels at nearly 300,000 kilometers per second requires more than a stopwatch. If you tried to time light over 20 meters with a manual timer you’d be trying to measure a delay of about 0.0000001 seconds—far faster than a human eye can blink.

To solve this we used the **Time-of-Flight (ToF)** method. Instead of a human observer we used a High-Speed Oscilloscope to "see" the pulse of light.



---

## How It Works: Total Internal Reflection

Optical fibers work like a hall of mirrors. Because the core has a higher refractive index than the cladding light undergoes **Total Internal Reflection (TIR)**.

$$ \sin\theta_c = \frac{n_{\text{cladding}}}{n_{\text{core}}} $$

In our experiment we used a **multi-mode fiber**. Unlike the thin fibers used in global internet backbones multi-mode fibers have a wider core allowing light to take multiple "paths" or modes. 

<Callout type="info">
  **Fun Fact:** Because light bounces at different angles in multi-mode fibers some "rays" actually travel a longer distance than the cable itself! This causes the pulse to spread out a phenomenon known as *Modal Dispersion*.
</Callout>



---

## The Setup: A Tale of Two Fibers

To get an accurate result we had to eliminate "system lag"—the time it takes for the electronics to wake up. We used a clever **Differential Measurement** technique:

1.  **The Reference:** We first sent a pulse through a 30 cm fiber. This gave us our "zero" time.
2.  **The Test:** We replaced it with an 18.9-meter fiber.
3.  **The Shift:** The difference in arrival time ($\Delta t$) is purely the time spent traveling the extra 18.6 meters of glass.

## The Results: Doing the Math

On the oscilloscope we observed a time shift of exactly **100 ns** ($100 \times 10^{-9}$ seconds). 

Using the basic formula $v = \frac{\Delta L}{\Delta t}$ we calculated:

$$ v_{\exp} = \frac{18.6\,\text{m}}{100 \times 10^{-9}\,\text{s}} = 1.86 \times 10^8\,\text{m/s} $$

To find the **Refractive Index ($n$)** we compared this to the speed of light in a vacuum ($c$):

$$ n_{\exp} = \frac{c}{v_{\exp}} \approx 1.61 $$

### So why the 10% difference?
The standard refractive index for silica is usually around $1.46$. Our result of $1.61$ was slightly higher. Why?
* **The Path Factor:** In multi-mode fibers light doesn't travel in a straight line; it zig-zags. The actual path is longer than the cable.
* **Human Error:** At a scale of 50ns per division on the oscilloscope a tiny 1mm shift in our cursor placement could change the result significantly.

---

## Why This Matters

Every time you load a webpage billions of pulses are doing exactly what we measured in the lab—bouncing through fibers at 186,000 kilometers per second. Understanding the limits of **pulse broadening** and **attenuation** is what allows engineers to build faster and more reliable internet for everyone.

Physics isn't just about abstract equations; it's about seeing those equations happen in real-time on a green screen in a dark lab in Istanbul.
