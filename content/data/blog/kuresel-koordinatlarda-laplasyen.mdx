## Motivation

In many physical systems—like the hydrogen atom in quantum mechanics or gravitational potentials in astrophysics—problems exhibit **spherical symmetry**. While the Laplacian $\nabla^2$ is simple in Cartesian coordinates ($\partial_x^2 + \partial_y^2 + \partial_z^2$), expressing it in spherical coordinates $(r, \theta, \phi)$ requires careful treatment of the unit vectors and partial derivatives.



---

## Defining the Del Operator

The gradient operator $\nabla$ in Cartesian components can be projected onto spherical basis functions. Let's define the components $A, B, C$ such that:

$$
\nabla = \hat{i} A + \hat{j} B + \hat{k} C
$$

Where the operator components are:

$$
\begin{aligned}
    A &= \sin\theta\cos\phi\frac{\partial}{\partial r} + \frac{\cos\theta\cos\phi}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} - \frac{\sin\phi}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial \phi} \\
    B &= \sin\theta\sin\phi\frac{\partial}{\partial r} + \frac{\cos\theta\sin\phi}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} + \frac{\cos\phi}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial \phi} \\
    C &= \cos\theta\frac{\partial}{\partial r} - \frac{\sin\theta}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}
\end{aligned}
$$

The Laplacian is defined as the dot product of the del operator with itself:

$$
\nabla^2 = \nabla \cdot \nabla = A^2 + B^2 + C^2
$$

---

## The Product Rule for Operators

The most critical step in this derivation is remembering that these are **operators**. When we compute $C^2$, the first operator acts on the *coefficients* of the second operator.

$$
(f \partial) \cdot (g \partial) = f \left( \frac{\partial g}{\partial \dots} \right) \partial + fg \frac{\partial^2}{\partial \dots^2}
$$

### Detailed Analysis of the $z$-component ($I_3$)

Let's look at $C \cdot C$:

$$
C \cdot C = \left( \cos\theta\frac{\partial}{\partial r} - \frac{\sin\theta}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \right) \left( \cos\theta\frac{\partial}{\partial r} - \frac{\sin\theta}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \right)
$$

Expanding this, we get:

1.  **Radial-Radial:** $\cos^2\theta \frac{\partial^2}{\partial r^2}$
2.  **Radial-Angular:** $\frac{\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} - \frac{\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial r \partial \theta}$
3.  **Angular-Angular:** $\frac{\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} + \frac{\sin^2\theta}{r^2} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial \theta^2}$

Summing these parts gives us $I_3$:

$$
I_3 = \cos^2\theta \frac{\partial^2}{\partial r^2} + \frac{\sin^2\theta}{r^2} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial \theta^2} - \frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial r \partial \theta} + \frac{\sin^2\theta}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} + \frac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{r^2} \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}
$$

---

## Final Result and Simplification

By summing $I_1 + I_2 + I_3$ and applying the identity $\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1$, the cross-derivatives cancel out, and we are left with the standard form:

$$
\nabla^2 = \frac{\partial^2}{\partial r^2} + \frac{2}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial r} + \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial \theta^2} + \frac{\cos\theta}{r^2\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} + \frac{1}{r^2\sin^2\theta}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial \phi^2}
$$

### Compact (Differential) Form

In most physics textbooks, the Laplacian is written in this elegant, compact form:

$$
\nabla^2 = \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r^2 \frac{\partial}{\partial r}\right) + \frac{1}{r^2\sin\theta}\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\left(\sin\theta \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\right) + \frac{1}{r^2\sin^2\theta}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial \phi^2}
$$

## Implementation in SymPy

If you want to verify these results symbolically, you can use Python's `sympy` library:

```python
from sympy import symbols, sin, cos, diff, Function
from sympy.vector import CoordSys3D

# Define the spherical coordinate system
N = CoordSys3D('N', transformation='spherical')
r, theta, phi = N.base_scalars()

# Define a scalar field f(r, theta, phi)
f = Function('f')(r, theta, phi)

# Compute Laplacian using built-in methods
from sympy.vector import Laplacian
laplacian_f = Laplacian(f)

print(laplacian_f.doit().simplify())

```
